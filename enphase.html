01

A late entrant with a lot to prove.

Enphase had strong brand trust in single-phase residential storage across Europe. The IQ Battery 5P was well established with installers in Germany, Austria, Switzerland, Netherlands, Belgium, and France. But three-phase storage — the segment preferred by larger homes and commercial installations — was dominated by established competitors.

The 3rd generation Enphase storage ecosystem, built around the IQ Battery 5P with FlexPhase architecture, was the product that would change that. My role was to ensure that when it launched, it launched with confidence — across markets, across channels, and across teams.

02

Field confidence would determine success, not feature completeness.

The temptation in a competitive product launch is to lead with specifications. We chose not to. Installers and distributors don't respond to datasheets — they respond to reliability narratives, supportability confidence, and ecosystem compatibility.

My bet was that if we could make installers feel genuinely confident explaining and supporting the product, adoption would follow. That meant investing heavily in enablement before we invested in reach.

There was also a harder call: when single-phase 5P inventory ran low before FlexPhase was ready, the decision was made to manufacture additional inventory rather than rush the launch. Short-term commercial pressure, long-term channel trust. That tradeoff shaped how I thought about everything else on the launch.

03

Readiness first. Scale second.

I sat at the intersection of technical readiness and commercial readiness — coordinating across product, engineering, sales, training, support, and regional marketing teams across 8 markets.

The work broke into four tracks:

Localization

Managing a pipeline from engineering review through translation agencies, local FAE validation, and web publishing across 25+ documents and 8 regions. I introduced AI-assisted drafting tools to shorten the path to reviewable quality without removing the technical accuracy checks that mattered for regulatory confidence.

Enablement

Building sales toolkits, training decks, messaging playbooks, and launch collateral that translated FlexPhase architecture into installer confidence narratives. The goal was to give every installer in every market the same ability to explain why this product was the right choice.

Beta program

Working with FAEs and training teams to secure qualified beta deployments in December, despite installer availability dropping during the holiday season. Field learnings from beta were fed directly back into messaging and launch readiness planning.

Regional sequencing

Prioritising markets based on grid standard compatibility, beta program confidence, and installer ecosystem maturity. Germany, Austria, and Switzerland first. Netherlands, Belgium, and France to follow.

04

A launch that held together under pressure.

The coordinated launch across multiple European regions completed on schedule despite timeline slippage earlier in the project. The decision to delay rather than rush preserved channel confidence during the transition from single-phase to three-phase — and that confidence showed up in how installers positioned the product in the field.

A few things I'd do differently: start localization optimization earlier in the content creation stage rather than using AI to accelerate smaller updates later. Build a standard multi-region launch playbook so coordination knowledge lives in a system rather than in people. Push for earlier joint planning between product, supply chain, and commercial teams so launch timing decisions are made with full visibility into channel realities.

The experience reinforced something I now treat as a principle: cross-functional clarity is a force multiplier. Most launch delays aren't caused by engineering quality — they're caused by misalignment between technical readiness and commercial readiness.
