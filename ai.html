AI & Ways of Working

AI as a working partner.

AI has genuinely changed what one person can build, analyse, and ship. Things that used to need a team — data pipelines, localised content at scale, functional prototypes, server infrastructure — are now within reach of a single person. This portfolio is a decent example of that.

The catch is that the tool getting more powerful doesn't mean it's getting wiser. I think about it through two analogies. A calculator given a pen — because I'm asking a computational model to do creative work, which means I need to stay close to what it's actually producing. And an autopilot given the cockpit — because no matter how capable the system, the final call has to be human. Both feel true at the same time.

The industry has a term for what happens when people stop paying attention — comprehension debt. It's real, and I've seen it. I haven't written production code in years, but when I build with AI I make sure I understand what's happening well enough to debug it when something goes sideways. And something always goes sideways eventually.

1.7M rows of field telemetry → data-backed marketing claims

Fleet battery data analysis

Traditional battery marketing is built around peak capacity and ideal conditions. But homes spend most of their time — up to 6,500 hours a year — running small loads like fridges, routers, and lights. This is precisely where conventional hybrid inverters are least efficient, dropping below 70% round-trip efficiency. Without field data, this was directionally understood but not proven at scale.

The analysis processed 1.7 million rows of real Enphase Enlighten telemetry to quantify how much time homes actually spend in low-load conditions, and at which power bands. That data became the evidentiary foundation for the PowerMatch positioning — a fleet-backed answer to the question: how bad is the low-load efficiency problem in practice? Automated cross-project status reporting with AI analysis

Jira sprint report summariser

Tracking launch readiness across documentation, compliance, web, and app teams meant manually checking dozens of tickets across different Jira projects. Information was scattered, patterns were invisible, and things slipped.

The tool pulls live Jira data and produces a structured status report with AI-generated analysis. It gives me three things: a single view across all projects and ticket states; early warning on which team is becoming a bottleneck before it becomes a blocker; and automatic surfacing of stale tickets — critical for compliance listings and grid whitelisting that can take 3–6 months and need consistent follow-up.
